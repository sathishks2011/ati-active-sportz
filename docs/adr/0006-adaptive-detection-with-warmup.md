# Adaptive motion detection with a Warm-up phase

Motion detection uses a per-pixel adaptive baseline (background-subtraction style) rather than a fixed pixel-difference threshold. Every Session opens with a ~15-second Warm-up phase during which the detector observes the Court ROI to establish "what idle looks like" in the current gym, lighting, and camera conditions. No Active Segments may open during Warm-up. After Warm-up, the detector triggers on significant deviation from the learned baseline. A "Skip Calibration" escape hatch falls back to a fixed-threshold detector for users who arrive mid-match.

Reason: fixed thresholds are fragile across gyms — a value that works in bright LED gyms with dark hardwood floors fails in dim fluorescent gyms with similar-colored players and floors, and outdoor courts have entirely different noise characteristics (HVAC drafts, autoexposure grain, distant motion). An adaptive baseline lets us ship one detector that handles diverse environments without per-user tuning, at the cost of needing ~15s of (roughly) idle ROI footage at the start of a Session. We considered the alternatives: (a) fixed threshold + no calibration, which loses too many gym/lighting environments; (b) explicit user-driven calibration ("clear the court for 10s"), which is impossible at most real tournaments where courts are constantly in motion. Implicit Warm-up assumes only that the *corners* of the ROI are roughly idle for a brief window — a much weaker assumption that holds in practice during pre-match warmups.

Hard to reverse because the Warm-up phase is user-visible (a "Calibrating…" indicator before recording can start) and the detection algorithm choice cascades into the entire detection pipeline. Surprising-without-context because a future engineer wondering "why doesn't this record for the first 15 seconds?" will need an answer.
